Autism Information Library

True and Full Agreement and Consent

The person taking the meds must agree with the diagnosis, be aware of the true implications of the meds, and make a choice free of coercion.

Agree with the diagnosis

The person taking the meds must agree with the diagnosis (not just be told that it is true) and have tried other things to get around it.

Be aware of the true implications of the meds

The person must know what the meds can and can't effectively be used for, what the possible side-effects are, and how to get off the meds if they want to stop at any time.

Choice free of coercion

The person must not be forced, coaxed, or coerced into taking a medication. It must always be an informed, educated choice. The person must not have continual "You should take medication" or even subtler but similar messages, bombarding them. The person must never be told "Medication is your only hope." Doctors, parents, and teachers must keep their own opinions about whether they want the person to take meds, out of the decision. They must be there to inform of possibilities, and there are always possibilities other than meds, so they must inform about those too, and inform about them first -- before medications are given as an option, and not demean or put down the other possibilities, or say that the other possibilities "are less likely to work." Another rule of thumb is that if the autistic must be coaxed into taking meds by being told the equivalent of "Oh, just try it for a certain trial period first" then that is coercion. Also, an echolalic or trained response is out of the question as far as consent" goes. That's not consent, that's training.
